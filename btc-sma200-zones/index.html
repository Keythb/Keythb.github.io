Methodology & data

Metric. Deviation = (Close ÷ 200-period SMA − 1) × 100. Zones are 5-percentage-point bands anchored at 0. "% of time" is the share of bars closing inside a band — 4h bars count 4 hours each, 1d bars count 24 hours. Deviation from a moving average is a descriptive statistic, not a forecast; historical time-in-zone does not imply a forward distribution.

Sources & vintage. The two series come from different venues, so prices differ slightly and the deviations are not strictly like-for-like. 0.39% of hours are absent from the Coinbase archive (scattered; largest gap 16h); 4h bars are built from available hours. Live spot polls Coinbase's keyless ticker and is measured against the cached SMA200 — a 200-period average moves ~1/200 per bar, so it is stable intraday. If the poll fails the panel falls back to the cached close and is marked SNAPSHOT. Figures derive from third-party data believed reliable but not independently verified, and are subject to revision without notice. Generated UTC.

Window. The primary view is a rolling years from , applied to both series from a common date so the two distributions stay directly comparable. It is the shortest window that clears every constraint at once: reachable by both series (the 4h archive only begins 2015-08), spanning two full cycles (2018 bear → 2021 top → 2022 bear → 2024+), excluding the pre-institutional 2011–13 era where the 1d deviation reached +1207%, and retaining enough independent swings for a stable interval on both — on the 4h, on the 1d. It is robust rather than knife-edge: sweeping the start from 6.5 to 9.5 years moves current-zone occupancy only ~17–19% (4h) and ~5–7% (1d), so nothing hinges on the exact date. Spot-ETF era and Full history bracket it on either side.

Sample size. Deviation from a 200-period SMA is heavily autocorrelated — consecutive bars are nearly the same observation — so the honest sample size is the number of times the series crosses its SMA, not the bar count. This is why the window cannot simply be shortened to taste: cutting the 1d series to one year leaves just 7 swings and no computable confidence interval, and its "% of time" would then describe where price happened to sit during a fraction of a single slow oscillation rather than a distribution. The 4h SMA200 spans ~33 days and the 1d ~200 days, so the 1d is roughly six times more constrained.

Freshness. The page rebuilds daily (05:15 UTC) from caches refreshed at 05:00. Spot is live on every load, but both 200 SMAs and all zone distributions are computed at build time. If a rebuild is missed the page says so at the top — it does not silently present stale averages as current.

Uncertainty. Because of that autocorrelation, raw "% of time" overstates precision. Every occupancy figure carries a 95% moving-block bootstrap CI (1,000 resamples; block = one SMA lookback), shown on the cards and as whiskers on the histogram. Read occupancy as a range. The intervals are wide — that is a property of the data, not a defect of the estimate, and it is what bounds how short the window can sensibly go.

Display. Each chart's axis spans , chosen per series and window because the 4h deviation lives inside ±50% while the 1d one carries a genuinely fat right tail. Zones outside that range are pooled into terminal buckets and the time series is clipped at that edge; the range is always widened to contain the current zone, so the NOW marker never lands in a pooled bucket. The Excel workbook retains every 5pp zone at full fidelity, uncapped.
