Bitcoin — 200 SMA Deviation Zones

How far price sits from its 200-period moving average, and how much of history it has spent there.

Methodology & data

Metric. Deviation = (Close ÷ 200-period SMA − 1) × 100. Zones are 5-percentage-point bands anchored at 0. "% of time" is the share of bars closing inside a band — 4h bars count 4 hours each, 1d bars count 24 hours. Deviation from a moving average is a descriptive statistic, not a forecast; historical time-in-zone does not imply a forward distribution.

Sources & vintage. The two series come from different venues, so prices differ slightly and the deviations are not strictly like-for-like. 0.39% of hours are absent from the Coinbase archive (scattered; largest gap 16h); 4h bars are built from available hours. Live spot polls Coinbase's keyless ticker and is measured against the cached SMA200 — a 200-period average moves ~1/200 per bar, so it is stable intraday. If the poll fails the panel falls back to the cached close and is marked SNAPSHOT. Figures derive from third-party data believed reliable but not independently verified, and are subject to revision without notice. Generated UTC.

Window. The 1d series reaches back to 2011; the 4h series only to 2015. Bitcoin's most extreme deviations occurred in 2011–2013, which only the 1d series observes — the full-history 1d distribution is skewed by an era of a thinner, pre-institutional market. Matched window puts both on common ground from .

Why the regime windows differ. Current regime uses a different start date per series, because the usable window is set by the metric's memory, not by the calendar. Deviation from a 200-period SMA is heavily autocorrelated — consecutive bars are nearly the same observation — so the honest sample size is the number of times the series crosses its SMA, not the bar count. The 4h SMA200 spans ~33 days and still registers swings since ; the 1d SMA200 spans ~200 days and registers only since . Cutting the 1d series to one year leaves just 7 swings and no computable confidence interval — its "% of time" would then describe where price happened to sit during a fraction of a single slow oscillation, not a distribution. These are the shortest windows each series actually supports.

Uncertainty. Because of that autocorrelation, raw "% of time" overstates precision. Every occupancy figure carries a 95% moving-block bootstrap CI (1,000 resamples; block = one SMA lookback), shown on the cards and as whiskers on the histogram. Read occupancy as a range. The intervals are wide — that is a property of the data, not a defect of the estimate, and it is the reason a shorter window was not chosen.

Display. Each chart's axis spans , chosen per series and window because the 4h deviation lives inside ±50% while the 1d one carries a genuinely fat right tail. Zones outside that range are pooled into terminal buckets and the time series is clipped at that edge; the range is always widened to contain the current zone, so the NOW marker never lands in a pooled bucket. The Excel workbook retains every 5pp zone at full fidelity, uncapped.
